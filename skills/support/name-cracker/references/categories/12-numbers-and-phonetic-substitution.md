# Numbers and phonetic substitution

Numbers and near-homophones stand in for words, letters, or entire syllables. The result is compact and technical-feeling, trading a little legibility for brevity and a distinctive silhouette.

## Techniques

- Letter-count numeronym: first letter + count of omitted letters + last letter (`i18n`, `k8s`, `a11y`, `o11y`)
- Phonetic numeronym: a digit replaces a syllable that sounds like it (`n8n`, `gr8`, `4chan`)
- Version or generation marker folded directly into the name (`Web3`, `IPv6`, `HTTP/2`)
- Alphanumeric mix: numerals sit beside words to signal quantity, date, version, or founder lore (`37signals`, `1Password`, `Formula 1`)
- Homophone substitution: a word is swapped for a similar-sounding letter, digit, or syllable string (`U2`, `Qlik`, `YouTube`)

## Examples

i18n, k8s, a11y, 1Password, 37signals, Qlik, U2, Web3

## Pitfalls

- Pronunciation and capitalization are often unclear on first encounter.
- Users may not know whether to type the digit literally or spell the word out.
- Can read as dated or be hard to dictate verbally once the trend fades.
